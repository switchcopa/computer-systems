

Practice Problem 1.1:
Suppose you work as a truck driver, and you have been hired to carry a load of
potatoes from Boise, Idaho, to Minneapolis, Minnesota, a total distance of 2,500
kilometers. You estimate you can average 100 km/hr driving within the speed
limits, requiring a total of 25 hours for the trip.

A. You hear on the news that Montana has just abolished its speed limit, which
constitutes 1,500 km of the trip. Your truck can travel at 150 km/hr. What
will be your speedup for the trip?
B. You can buy a new turbocharger for your truck at www.fasttrucks.com. They
stock a variety of models, but the faster you want to go, the more it will cost.
How fast must you travel through Montana to get an overall speedup for
your trip of 1.67×?

Solution:
A. We need to get the underlying variables in the speedup formula for the trip.
First, the component being enhanced is the speed of the portion of the trip
that takes place in Montana.
Next, we look at the time dependent factor we would name f. In the original
case, to cover 1500km along Montana at 100km/h, we would need
1500/100 = 25 hours.
Therefore, the time dependent improvement factor f is:
f = 15 hours/25 hours = 0.6.

The performance ratio here is k, which is the ratio between the
new speed (150km/h) and the old speed (100km/h), thus:
k = 150/100 = 1.5.
Hence, we have the variables for the speedup formula, and:

S = 1/[(1-f) + f/k] = 1.25

B. 
S = 1/[(1-f) + f/k]
1-f+f/k = 1/S
1 - f(1-1/k) = 1/S
f(1-1/k) = 1-1/S
1-1/k = 1/f(1-1/S)
1/k = 1/f(f - (1 - 1/S))

Hence: k = f/(f - (1 - 1/S)) approximately 3
